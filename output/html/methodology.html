Technical Methodology

How we estimate energy savings from Occupancy-Driven Control Ventilation (O-DCV) for 22,948 commercial buildings.

What Is O-DCV?

The Problem: Ventilating Empty Space

Every commercial building has a ventilation system that brings in fresh outdoor air. Building codes specify how much fresh air each space needs, typically measured in CFM per person. The problem: Most buildings ventilate based on how many people the space was designed for, not how many are actually there.

Consider a typical office floor designed for 200 people:

  • The ventilation system delivers fresh air for 200 people
  • But only 120 show up (hybrid work means 40% work from home)
  • And 3 floors are completely vacant (no tenant)
  • The building pumps fresh air for 800 people who don't exist

That fresh outdoor air isn't free:

  • In winter: 20°F outside, 70°F inside — every CFM must be heated
  • In summer: 95°F/humid outside, 72°F/dry inside — every CFM must be cooled and dehumidified

Ventilating empty space is pure waste.

The Solution: O-DCV

O-DCV (Occupancy-Driven Control Ventilation) uses real-time occupancy sensors to answer: How many people are actually in this zone right now? Then it adjusts ventilation to match:

  • Conference room for 20 people but only 4 present? Deliver air for 4.
  • Entire floor empty at 6pm? Reduce to minimum setback.
  • Big meeting happening? Ramp up for actual headcount.

Where the Savings Come From

O-DCV reduces energy consumption in three ways:

ComponentHow It Saves EnergyTypical Share
Fan EnergyFans run slower when less air is needed (fan affinity laws: half the airflow = 1/8 the energy)20-30%
Heating EnergyLess cold outdoor air to heat in winter40-50%
Cooling EnergyLess hot/humid outdoor air to cool and dehumidify in summer30-40%

Opportunity by Building Type

Vacancy vs. Utilization: Two Types of Empty Space

Understanding the difference between vacancy and utilization is critical:

  • Vacancy = Unleased space (empty suites with no tenant). Only applies to multi-tenant buildings like offices.
  • Utilization = People actually present in occupied space. A fully-leased office with 50% work-from-home has 0% vacancy but only 50% utilization.

Technical Detail: Opportunity Score Formula

Measures how much conditioned space is empty and available for ventilation reduction.

Multi-tenant buildings (office, medical office, mixed use):
Vacant space has no tenant but is still ventilated. Leased space may be underutilized due to hybrid work.

Opportunity = Vacancy + (1 - Vacancy) × (1 - Utilization)

Example: 25% vacancy + 75% leased at 55% utilization = 25% + (75% × 45%) = 59% opportunity.

Single-operator buildings (hotel, school, retail):
No vacancy concept—the operator controls all space. Opportunity driven purely by when space is empty.

Opportunity = 1 - Utilization

Example: School at 45% utilization (summers, nights, weekends off) = 55% opportunity.

Limited-opportunity buildings (hospital, lab, residential care):
Code-mandated ventilation for infection control, fume hoods, or 24/7 residents limits savings to ~30% of theoretical.

Opportunity = (1 - Utilization) × 0.3

Calculating Savings

1. Measure the opportunity

Using vacancy and/or utilization data, calculate how much conditioned space is actually empty.

2. Convert energy usage to energy cost

Buildings report energy consumption (kWh, therms, Mlb steam). We convert this to cost using real utility rates sourced by zip code from EIA data and local utility tariff filings:

Electricity has two cost components:

  • Energy charges: Based on total kWh consumed. We apply a 1.10× multiplier to account for taxes, distribution charges, transmission fees, and surcharges that appear on real utility bills beyond the base commodity rate.
  • Demand charges: Based on peak kW draw. Commercial buildings pay for their highest 15-minute demand each month. We estimate peak demand from annual consumption using load factor. We apply a 1.265× multiplier to account for demand ratchet clauses (paying for historical peaks), seasonal rate differentials, and power factor penalties.

Load Factors

Load factor = average demand / peak demand. Higher load factor means more even energy use throughout the day:

Building TypeLoad FactorWhy
Hospital (24/7)0.60 – 0.70Continuous operation, some peaks
Office0.40 – 0.50Peaks during business hours
Retail0.35 – 0.45Peaks during open hours
Warehouse0.30 – 0.40Variable operations
Default0.45Used when building type unknown

Natural Gas: Convert kBtu to therms (÷100), multiply by rate, apply 1.10× for delivery charges and taxes.

District Steam: Convert kBtu to Mlb (÷909), multiply by rate. No adder—steam utilities like ConEd bundle all charges into the rate.

Fuel Oil: Convert kBtu to MMBtu (÷1000), multiply by rate, apply 1.10× for delivery and taxes.

Technical Detail: Energy Cost Formulas

Utility rates from EIA State Electricity Data and NREL OpenEI Utility Rate Database.

Electricity (two components):

Peak_kW = Annual_kWh ÷ (8,760 hours × Load_Factor)
Energy_Cost = kWh × Rate_kWh × 1.10
Demand_Cost = Peak_kW × Rate_kW × 12 months × 1.265
  • 1.10× energy multiplier: Accounts for taxes, distribution charges, transmission fees, and surcharges beyond base commodity rate (typically 8-15% on real bills).
  • 1.265× demand multiplier: Accounts for demand ratchet clauses (paying for historical peaks), seasonal rate differentials (summer 20-40% higher), and power factor penalties.
  • Load factor: Ratio of average to peak demand. Default 0.45 (office); hospitals 0.60-0.70; warehouses 0.30-0.40.

Natural Gas:

Gas_Cost = (kBtu ÷ 100) × Rate_therm × 1.10

Conversion: 1 therm = 100 kBtu. 1.10× multiplier for delivery charges and taxes.

District Steam:

Steam_Cost = (kBtu ÷ 909) × Rate_Mlb

Conversion: 1 Mlb (thousand lbs) ≈ 909 kBtu. No multiplier—steam rates from utilities like ConEd are all-inclusive.

Fuel Oil:

Fuel_Oil_Cost = (kBtu ÷ 1000) × Rate_MMBtu × 1.10

Technical Detail: HVAC Percentages

Base values from EIA CBECS 2018 national survey of 6,436 commercial buildings, cross-referenced by building type and climate zone.

Adjustments applied to each building:

  • ENERGY STAR score: ±5% (efficient buildings use less HVAC share)
  • Year built: ±4% (older buildings have less efficient envelopes)
  • EUI vs peer median: ±6% (high-EUI buildings often have HVAC issues)
  • Combined adjustment capped at ±12%

Electric HVAC minimum: 15%
Ventilation fans (8-12%), chilled water pumps (2-4%), condenser pumps (1-3%), and controls (1-2%) run continuously regardless of occupancy.

Special cases by building type:

  • Hotels: Only 21.5% of gas → HVAC. The rest: ~42% domestic hot water, ~33% cooking.
  • Restaurants: Only 19.9% of gas → HVAC. The rest: ~72% commercial cooking.
  • Supermarkets: Electric HVAC only 20% (refrigeration excluded from savings calculations).
  • Offices: 92% of gas → HVAC (space heating dominates), 47% of electricity → HVAC.

Technical Detail: Master Formula

ODCV% = Floor + (Opportunity × Automation × Range) × EfficiencyMod × ClimateMod

Where Range = Ceiling - Floor for each building type.

Component breakdown:

  • Floor/Ceiling: Building-type-specific min/max savings (e.g., Office 20-40%, Hospital 5-15%)
  • Opportunity: 0-1 score based on vacancy and utilization
  • Automation: Average of year-built score (0-1) and size score (0-1)
  • Efficiency Modifier: 0.85 for ENERGY STAR 90+ (less waste), 1.10 for ES <25 (more waste)
  • Climate Modifier: 1.10 Northern, 1.05 North-Central, 1.00 South-Central, 0.95 Southern

Example: Office (20-40% range), 59% opportunity, 0.875 automation, ES score 68 (1.0×), North-Central (1.05×):
ODCV% = 20% + (59% × 0.875 × 20%) × 1.0 × 1.05 = 20% + 10.8% = 30.8%

Technical Detail: Annual Savings Calculation

ODCV reduces energy consumption for each fuel type proportional to its HVAC share.

Step 1: Calculate HVAC cost by fuel type

Elec_HVAC_cost = Elec_cost × hvac_pct_elec
Gas_HVAC_cost = Gas_cost × hvac_pct_gas
Steam_HVAC_cost = Steam_cost × hvac_pct_steam
Total_HVAC_cost = Elec + Gas + Steam + Fuel_Oil

Step 2: Apply ODCV savings percentage

Annual_savings = Total_HVAC_cost × ODCV%

Example: Building with $500K total energy, 45% to HVAC ($225K), 30% ODCV savings = $67,500/year.

Technical Detail: BPS Fine Calculations

Each city calculates fines differently. ODCV reduces energy → reduces emissions → reduces or avoids fines.

  • NYC Local Law 97: $268/tCO2e over emissions cap (0.00758 tCO2e/sqft for office).
  • Boston BERDO 2.0: $234/tCO2e Alternative Compliance Payment over cap (0.0053 tCO2e/sqft).
  • Cambridge BEUDO: $234/tCO2e, but target is 20% below building's own baseline.
  • DC BEPS: Up to $10/sqft (max $7.5M), prorated by gap from ENERGY STAR target.
  • Denver Energize: $0.15/kBtu over EUI target.
  • Seattle BEPS: $10/sqft per 5-year cycle if over 0.00081 tCO2e/sqft.
  • St. Louis BEPS: $500/day non-compliance if EUI exceeds ~65 kBtu/sqft target.
Fine_avoided = Fine_baseline - Fine_postODCV

Technical Detail: Property Valuation (Income Capitalization)

Commercial real estate is valued on Net Operating Income (NOI). Every $1 of operating expense avoided increases NOI by $1, which increases property value via the capitalization rate.

Value_increase = (Utility_savings + Fine_avoidance) / Cap_rate

Example: $100K annual savings ÷ 7% cap rate = $1.43M property value increase.

Cap rate interpretation: Lower cap rates (5-6%) mean higher value multipliers (16-20×). Higher cap rates (8-10%) mean lower multipliers (10-12×). Riskier assets command higher cap rates.

Technical Detail: ENERGY STAR Score Improvement

ENERGY STAR scores compare a building's energy use to similar buildings nationwide. A score of 50 means median performance; 75+ qualifies for certification.

How scores are calculated:
EPA uses a gamma distribution regression model that maps source EUI to percentile rank, normalized for weather, operating hours, and building characteristics. See EPA Technical Reference.

Estimating post-ODCV score:

New_EUI = Current_EUI × (1 - ODCV% × Weighted_HVAC%)

Lower EUI → higher percentile → higher score. A 10% EUI reduction typically improves score by 5-15 points depending on starting position on the distribution curve.

Validation & Limitations

Why This Methodology Is Defensible

  • Building-level data: All calculations use actual energy consumption from official city benchmarking disclosures (NYC LL84, LA, San Francisco, Seattle, etc.) and EPA Portfolio Manager—not estimates or averages.
  • Peer-reviewed sources: HVAC percentages from EIA CBECS 2018 (6,436 buildings surveyed). Vacancy from CBRE/CommercialEdge. Utilization from Kastle badge-swipe data.
  • Conservative assumptions:
    • Floor savings (20%) achievable even with minimal implementation
    • Limited-opportunity buildings (hospitals, labs) use 0.3× multiplier
    • Combined adjustment capped at ±12% to prevent outliers
  • Verified formulas: Energy cost calculations validated against 22,948 buildings with known costs (match rate: 100%, max deviation: <0.01%).

Known Limitations

  • HVAC controllability varies: Buildings with pneumatic controls, split systems, or no BMS may achieve lower savings than predicted. We use year-built as a proxy for automation capability.
  • Occupancy data lag: Vacancy rates are quarterly; utilization is monthly. Rapid market changes may not be reflected.
  • Fuel type assumptions: Hotels and restaurants have lower gas HVAC percentages due to cooking/DHW. These are building-type averages; individual buildings may differ.
  • BPS Year 1 only: Fine calculations use 2024-2029 caps. Future periods have stricter limits—actual fine avoidance may be higher in later years.
  • Property valuations are indicative: Actual values depend on location, condition, lease terms, tenant quality, and market conditions beyond energy costs.
  • Geographic coverage: Dataset covers 12 states with disclosure laws. Buildings in non-disclosure jurisdictions are not included.
