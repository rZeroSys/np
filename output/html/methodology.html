Technical Methodology

How Nationwide Prospector calculates ODCV savings, BPS fine avoidance, and property value impact.

The Problem

Commercial buildings are designed for peak occupancy that rarely occurs. A conference room for 20 sits empty most of the day. An office floor leased to 200 has 80 present. A school closes for summer but HVAC runs for maintenance.

HVAC systems ventilate, heat, and cool based on design capacity—not actual presence. The result: buildings condition vast amounts of empty space.

The Solution

ODCV (Occupancy-Driven Control Ventilation) uses sensors to detect real-time occupancy and adjusts ventilation accordingly. When a floor is empty, airflow drops. When a conference room fills, ventilation increases. The building breathes with its occupants instead of running at full blast 24/7.

This isn't theoretical—it's measured. We calculate savings using actual vacancy rates, utilization data, and energy benchmarks for each building.

How Opportunity Varies by Building Type

Different buildings have different emptiness patterns. The method for measuring opportunity depends on ownership and operations:

Building Type Method Why
Office, Medical Office, Mixed Use Vacancy + Utilization Multi-tenant: unleased space + leased space where tenants aren't showing up
Hotel Utilization Rooms booked or not; guests out during day for meetings, sightseeing
K-12 School, Higher Ed Utilization Schedule-driven: empty summers, holidays, evenings, weekends
Retail, Supermarket, Mall Utilization Traffic swings between peak and empty; designed for peak
Theater, Venue, Event Space Utilization Spaces for thousands sit empty most of the year
Restaurant/Bar Utilization (dining only) Kitchen exhaust must run; savings from dining areas between rushes
Gym/Fitness Utilization Sharp AM/PM peaks, near-empty midday and overnight
Library/Museum Utilization Climate runs 24/7 for preservation; visitors only during limited hours
Hospital, Laboratory Utilization (dampened) Code-mandated ventilation; savings in non-clinical areas only
Residential Care Utilization (dampened) Residents on-site 24/7 unlike hotel guests
Data Center None Cooling is for equipment heat, not people

Vacancy = unleased space (empty suites). Only applies to multi-tenant buildings.

Utilization = people actually present in occupied space. A fully-leased office with 50% WFH has 0% vacancy but 50% utilization.

Why Cities Differ

The same building type shows different savings potential in different cities:

  • Vacancy varies by market: SF offices have higher vacancy than NYC, so SF shows more opportunity
  • Return-to-office culture differs: Texas/Midwest have stronger in-person work than Bay Area/Seattle
  • Climate affects HVAC load: Northern heating-dominated climates show slightly higher savings than Southern cooling-dominated ones
  • Grid emissions vary: Seattle hydro means less carbon per kWh saved than Midwest coal grids

From Opportunity to Dollar Savings

1. Measure the opportunity

Using vacancy and/or utilization data, calculate how much conditioned space is actually empty.1

2. Determine HVAC share of energy

Not all energy goes to HVAC. We use federal survey data to estimate what fraction of electricity, gas, and steam goes to heating/cooling/ventilation for each building type.2

3. Apply modifiers

Building age and size affect control sophistication. Current efficiency and climate zone also adjust the estimate.3

4. Calculate annual savings

Apply the ODCV savings percentage to the HVAC portion of utility costs.4

Beyond Utility Savings

BPS Fine Avoidance

Seven cities penalize buildings exceeding emissions or energy targets: NYC (LL97), Boston (BERDO), Cambridge (BEUDO), Washington DC, Denver, Seattle, St. Louis.

ODCV reduces energy → reduces emissions → reduces fines.5

Property Value Impact

Commercial real estate is valued on Net Operating Income. Every dollar of OpEx avoided increases NOI by a dollar. Value increase = annual savings ÷ cap rate.6

ENERGY STAR Score Improvement

Lower energy consumption improves ENERGY STAR score. We estimate post-ODCV scores using EPA methodology.7

Technical Formulas

1 Opportunity Score
Multi-tenant (office, MOB, mixed use):
Opportunity = Vacancy + (1 - Vacancy) × (1 - Utilization)
Single-operator (hotel, school, retail):
Opportunity = 1 - Utilization
Limited-opportunity (hospital, lab):
Opportunity = (1 - Utilization) × 0.3
2 HVAC Percentages
From CBECS 2018, adjusted for ENERGY STAR score, year built, and EUI vs peers. Electric HVAC minimum 15% (fans/pumps always run). Hotels: ~20% of gas to HVAC (rest is DHW + cooking). Restaurants: ~18% gas HVAC (72% cooking).
3 Master Formula
ODCV% = Floor + (Opportunity × Automation × (Ceiling - Floor)) × EfficiencyMod × ClimateMod
Automation = avg of year-built and size scores. Efficiency: 0.85 for ES 90+, up to 1.10 for ES <25. Climate: 1.10 Northern to 0.95 Southern.
4 Annual Savings
HVAC_cost = Σ(fuel_cost × hvac_pct)
Annual_savings = HVAC_cost × ODCV%
5 BPS Fines
NYC LL97: $268/tCO2e over cap. Boston/Cambridge: $234/tCO2e. DC: up to $10/sqft. Denver: $0.30/kBtu over target.
Fine_avoided = Fine_baseline - Fine_postODCV
6 Valuation
Value_increase = (Utility_savings + Fine_avoidance) / Cap_rate
Cap rates: Office 7.5%, Hotel 8.0%, Retail 6.25%, Medical 7.5%.
7 ENERGY STAR
Scores map EUI to percentile via gamma distribution.
New_EUI = Current_EUI × (1 - ODCV% × Weighted_HVAC%)
