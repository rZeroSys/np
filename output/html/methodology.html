The Core Problem

Commercial buildings are designed for peak occupancy that rarely occurs. A conference room built for 20 people sits empty most of the day. An office floor leased to 200 workers has 80 present. A school closes for summer but HVAC runs for maintenance.

The result: buildings condition vast amounts of empty space. HVAC systems ventilate, heat, and cool based on design capacity, not actual presence.

ODCV (Occupancy-Driven Control Ventilation) recovers this waste by adjusting ventilation to match real-time occupancy instead of theoretical maximums.

How Opportunity Varies by Building Type

Different buildings have different patterns of emptiness. The method for measuring savings opportunity depends on ownership structure and operational patterns:

Building Type Method Why This Method
Office, Medical Office, Mixed Use Vacancy + Utilization Multi-tenant buildings have two sources of waste: unleased space still being conditioned, plus leased space where tenants aren't showing up
Hotel Utilization Single operator. Rooms are booked or not, and guests leave during the day for meetings, sightseeing, dining
K-12 School, Higher Ed Utilization Schedule-driven. Empty during summer, holidays, evenings, weekends—but HVAC often runs continuously
Retail, Supermarket, Mall Utilization Customer traffic swings between peak and empty. Buildings designed for peak but conditioned constantly
Theater, Venue, Event Space Utilization Event-driven. Spaces for thousands sit empty most of the year but remain conditioned
Restaurant/Bar Utilization (dining only) Kitchen exhaust must run during cooking. Savings come from dining areas between meal rushes
Gym/Fitness Utilization Sharp peaks at morning and evening, near-empty midday and overnight
Library/Museum Utilization Climate control runs 24/7 for preservation, but visitors only present during limited hours
Hospital, Laboratory Utilization (dampened) Code-mandated ventilation limits reductions. Savings mostly in non-clinical areas like waiting rooms and admin offices
Residential Care Utilization (dampened) Unlike hotels, residents live on-site 24/7. Minimal empty periods to recover
Data Center None Cooling is for equipment heat, not people. Human occupancy is irrelevant

Vacancy = unleased space (empty suites, no tenant). Only applies to multi-tenant buildings.

Utilization = how much of occupied space has people present. Applies to all buildings. A fully-leased office with 50% work-from-home has 0% vacancy but 50% utilization.

Why Cities Differ

The same building type in different cities shows different savings potential:

  • Vacancy rates vary by market: San Francisco offices have higher vacancy than NYC, so SF buildings show more opportunity from unleased space
  • Return-to-office culture differs: Cities with stronger in-person work (Texas, Midwest) show lower utilization-based opportunity than cities with more remote work (Bay Area, Seattle)
  • Climate affects heating/cooling balance: Northern climates with more heating load show slightly higher HVAC savings potential than cooling-dominated Southern climates
  • Grid emissions determine carbon impact: Seattle's hydroelectric grid means the same kWh reduction produces less carbon savings than in coal-heavy Midwest grids

Data sources: CBRE Office Figures (vacancy), Kastle Back to Work Barometer (utilization), EPA eGRID (grid emissions).

From Opportunity to Dollar Savings

1. Measure the opportunity

Using vacancy and/or utilization data (depending on building type), we calculate how much conditioned space is actually empty.1

2. Determine HVAC share of energy

Not all building energy goes to HVAC. We use CBECS survey data to estimate what fraction of electricity, gas, and steam goes to heating, cooling, and ventilation for each building type.2

3. Apply modifiers

Building age and size affect how sophisticated existing controls are (newer, larger buildings likely have better BMS). Current efficiency (ENERGY STAR score) and climate zone also adjust the estimate.3

4. Calculate annual savings

The ODCV savings percentage (typically 20-40% for offices) is applied to the HVAC portion of utility costs to get annual dollar savings.4

Beyond Utility Savings

BPS Fine Avoidance

Seven cities have Building Performance Standards that penalize buildings exceeding emissions or energy targets: NYC (LL97), Boston (BERDO), Cambridge (BEUDO), Washington DC, Denver, Seattle, and St. Louis.

ODCV reduces energy consumption, which reduces emissions, which can reduce or eliminate these fines. We calculate baseline fines (without ODCV) and post-ODCV fines for each applicable building.5

Source: IMT BPS Comparison Matrix

Property Value Impact

Commercial real estate is valued based on Net Operating Income (NOI). Every dollar of operating expense avoided—whether utility savings or fine avoidance—increases NOI by a dollar.

Value increase = annual savings ÷ cap rate. At a 7% cap rate, $100,000 in annual savings adds $1.43M to property value.6

Source: CBRE Cap Rate Survey

ENERGY STAR Score Improvement

Reduced energy consumption lowers EUI, which improves ENERGY STAR score. We estimate post-ODCV scores using EPA's scoring methodology.7

Technical Formulas

1 Opportunity Score
Multi-tenant (office, medical office, mixed use):
Opportunity = Vacancy + (1 - Vacancy) × (1 - Utilization)
Single-operator (hotel, school, retail):
Opportunity = 1 - Utilization
Limited-opportunity (hospital, lab):
Opportunity = (1 - Utilization) × 0.3
2 HVAC Percentages
From CBECS 2018, adjusted for ENERGY STAR score, year built, and EUI vs peers. Electric HVAC minimum 15% (fans, pumps, controls always run). Hotels: only ~20% of gas goes to HVAC (rest is DHW + cooking). Restaurants: ~18% gas HVAC (72% cooking).
3 Master Formula
ODCV% = Floor + (Opportunity × Automation × (Ceiling - Floor)) × EfficiencyMod × ClimateMod
Automation = average of year-built score and size score. Efficiency modifier based on ENERGY STAR bands (0.85 for 90+, up to 1.10 for <25). Climate modifier: 1.10 Northern, 1.05 North-Central, 1.00 South-Central, 0.95 Southern.
4 Annual Savings
HVAC_cost = Σ(fuel_cost × hvac_pct) for each fuel
Annual_savings = HVAC_cost × ODCV%
5 BPS Fine Calculation
Varies by city. NYC LL97: $268/tCO2e over cap. Boston/Cambridge: $234/tCO2e. DC: up to $10/sqft based on ENERGY STAR gap. Denver: $0.30/kBtu over EUI target. Seattle: emissions intensity target.
Fine_avoided = Fine_baseline - Fine_postODCV
6 Valuation Impact
Value_increase = (Utility_savings + Fine_avoidance) / Cap_rate
Cap rates by type: Office 7.5%, Hotel 8.0%, Retail 6.25%, Medical 7.5%.
7 ENERGY STAR Estimation
ENERGY STAR scores map EUI to percentile via gamma distribution. We calculate post-ODCV EUI, convert to efficiency ratio, then to score.
New_EUI = Current_EUI × (1 - ODCV% × Weighted_HVAC%)
