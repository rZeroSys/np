Technical Methodology

Opportunity by Building Type

Vacancy vs. Utilization: Two Types of Empty Space

Understanding the difference between vacancy and utilization is critical:

  • Vacancy = Unleased space (empty suites with no tenant). Only applies to multi-tenant buildings like offices.
  • Utilization = People actually present in occupied space. A fully-leased office with 50% work-from-home has 0% vacancy but only 50% utilization.

Three Opportunity Formulas

Different building types use different formulas based on how they operate:

Building CategoryFormulaExample
Multi-tenant
(Office, Medical Office, Mixed Use)		 Opportunity = Vacancy + (1 - Vacancy) × (1 - Utilization) 25% vacancy + 75% leased at 46% utilization = 25% + (75% × 54%) = 66% opportunity
Single-operator
(Hotel, School, Retail, Venue)		 Opportunity = 1 - Utilization School at 75% utilization = 25% opportunity
Limited-opportunity
(Lab, Residential Care)		 Opportunity = (1 - Utilization) × 0.3 Residential Care at 75% utilization: (1 - 0.75) × 0.3 = 7.5% opportunity

K-12 School 180 school days × 11 hrs HVAC / 8,760 hrs = 22.6% base; adjusted by state for year-round programs (CA, AZ higher) Schools have fixed calendars set by law. No "vacancy" concept - district owns all classrooms. Empty summers (12 weeks), holidays, nights, weekends = huge ODCV opportunity.
Higher Ed 32 weeks in session × 35% classroom utilization + HVAC buffer; varies by institution type (research vs teaching) University owns all space - no tenants. Classrooms scheduled for specific classes, empty the rest. APPA studies show only 30-40% of available hours have classes. 20 weeks/year of breaks.
Hotel Room Occupancy × 45% Guest Presence Factor (from DOE studies) Hotel owns all rooms - no "vacancy". But a booked room ≠ occupied room. Guests are OUT most of the day (business, sightseeing). DOE: guests in-room only ~10 hrs/day = 45% presence.
Retail Traffic-weighted: morning lull (15-20%) + lunch rush (60-70%) + afternoon (20-30%) + evening rush (60-80%) Store owns/leases entire space - no sub-tenants. Designed for peak Black Friday capacity, but "50% of week's traffic in busiest 20 hours" = 148 other hours much emptier.
Supermarket Traffic-weighted by hour: peak (4 hrs × 90%) + moderate (8 hrs × 50%) + slow (6 hrs × 20%); 24/7 stores average lower Single operator (Kroger, Safeway, etc). Extended hours = more empty time. Peak 5-7pm is 80-100% capacity; overnight is 5-15%. Refrigeration separate from HVAC.
Wholesale Club Zone-weighted: Sales floor (60% of bldg) × 48% traffic + Stock areas (40% of bldg) × 10% = 33% Single operator (Costco, Sam's, BJ's). 30-40% of building is back-of-house stock rooms with only forklift drivers. Shorter hours (10am-8:30pm), weekend-heavy traffic.
Inpatient Hospital Zone-weighted by area: patient rooms (40% @ 70%), clinical/OR (20% @ 85%), waiting (10% @ 30%), exam (10% @ 35%), admin (10% @ 40%), cafe (10% @ 35%) Hospital owns entire building. Not just patient rooms - huge non-clinical areas (waiting rooms, exam rooms, admin) get medical-grade ventilation 24/7 but are often empty.
Specialty Hospital High base occupancy (72-82%) with 0.3× dampening multiplier due to code-mandated minimum ventilation 24/7 operation with strict infection control. ASHRAE 170 MANDATES minimum air changes regardless of occupancy - can't reduce below code. Opportunity limited to non-critical areas only.
Outpatient Clinic Exam room occupancy (~25% actual patient time) × business hours (weekdays 8-5 = 30% of week) Clinic owns/operates space. Patients in exam room only 15-30 min per appointment. Between patients: room empty but ventilated at medical-grade rates. Closed evenings/weekends.
Residential Care Room Occupancy × 95% Resident Presence Factor Unlike hotel guests who leave for sightseeing, residents LIVE there 24/7. They eat, sleep, socialize on-site. Only ~5% time away for doctor visits. High occupancy + high presence = limited opportunity.
Theater 8 shows/week × 3 hrs = 24 hrs shows + 11 hrs pre/post/rehearsal = 35 hrs HVAC / 168 hrs × 70% avg capacity Theater owns venue. Seats 2,000+ people but only 8 shows/week. Empty 80% of the time but must maintain climate for equipment, costumes, and pre-show conditioning.
Venue 60-80 events/year × 5 hrs + setup/teardown = ~550 hrs / 8,760 hrs = 6% of year active Arena owns venue. 41 NBA games + 20 concerts + 20 other = 81 events/year. Building designed for 20,000 people sits EMPTY 93% of the year. Massive ODCV opportunity.
Library/Museum Open 50 hrs/week (30% of time) × 40% average visitor density during open hours Public institution owns building. HVAC runs 24/7 for collection preservation (temperature/humidity for artifacts) but visitors only come during limited public hours. Climate for objects, not people.
Restaurant/Bar Dining area (60% of bldg) traffic-weighted by meal period; kitchen (40% of bldg) EXCLUDED from ODCV Restaurant owns space. Kitchen exhaust hoods MUST run at full blast during all cooking hours (fire/health code). ODCV opportunity ONLY in dining area, between meal rushes when seats empty.

Calculating Savings

1. Measure the opportunity

Using vacancy and/or utilization data, calculate how much conditioned space is actually empty.

2. Convert energy usage to energy cost

Buildings report energy consumption (kWh, therms, Mlb steam). We convert this to cost using real utility rates sourced by zip code from EIA data and local utility tariff filings:

Electricity has two cost components:

  • Energy charges: Based on total kWh consumed. We apply a 1.10× multiplier to account for taxes, distribution charges, transmission fees, and surcharges that appear on real utility bills beyond the base commodity rate.
  • Demand charges: Based on peak kW draw. Commercial buildings pay for their highest 15-minute demand each month. We estimate peak demand from annual consumption using load factor. We apply a 1.265× multiplier to account for demand ratchet clauses (paying for historical peaks), seasonal rate differentials, and power factor penalties.

Load Factors

Load factor = average demand / peak demand. Higher load factor means more even energy use throughout the day:

Building TypeLoad FactorWhy
Hospital (24/7)0.60 – 0.70Continuous operation, some peaks
Office0.40 – 0.50Peaks during business hours
Retail0.35 – 0.45Peaks during open hours
Warehouse0.30 – 0.40Variable operations
Default0.45Used when building type unknown

Natural Gas: Convert kBtu to therms (÷100), multiply by rate, apply 1.10× for delivery charges and taxes.

District Steam: Convert kBtu to Mlb (÷909), multiply by rate. No adder—steam utilities like ConEd bundle all charges into the rate.

Fuel Oil: Convert kBtu to MMBtu (÷1000), multiply by rate, apply 1.10× for delivery and taxes.

*Why Hotels and Restaurants differ: In most buildings, nearly all gas goes to space heating. But hotels use gas for laundry, guest hot water, and restaurant kitchens. Restaurants use most gas for commercial cooking. ODCV only saves on the HVAC portion—you can't reduce guest hot water by sensing occupancy.

Adjustments applied per-building: Energy Star score (±5%), Year built (±4%), EUI vs peers (±6%). Electric HVAC has 15% minimum floor (fans/pumps always run). Combined adjustment capped at ±12%.

Apply the ODCV savings percentage to the HVAC portion of utility costs.

Technical Detail: Master Formula

ODCV% = (Floor + Opportunity × Automation × Range) × EfficiencyMod × ClimateMod

Where Range = Ceiling - Floor for each building type. Modifiers apply to the entire calculation.

Component breakdown:

  • Floor/Ceiling: Building-type-specific min/max HVAC savings (e.g., Office 20-45%, Hospital 20-43%)
  • Opportunity: 0-1 score based on vacancy and utilization
  • Automation: Weighted combination: 50% year-built score + 25% size score + 25% zip income tier
  • Efficiency Modifier: 0.85 for ENERGY STAR 90+ (less waste), 1.10 for ES <25 (more waste)
  • Climate Modifier: 1.10 Northern, 1.05 North-Central, 1.00 South-Central, 0.95 Southern

Example: Office (floor 20%, ceiling 45%) with 50% opportunity, 0.80 automation score (2015 building in high-income zip), average efficiency (1.0×), baseline climate (1.0×): (20% + 50% × 0.80 × 25%) × 1.0 × 1.0 = 30% HVAC savings.

Technical Detail: Annual Savings Calculation

ODCV reduces energy consumption for each fuel type proportional to its HVAC share.

Step 1: Calculate HVAC cost by fuel type

Elec_HVAC_cost = Elec_cost × hvac_pct_elec
Gas_HVAC_cost = Gas_cost × hvac_pct_gas
Steam_HVAC_cost = Steam_cost × hvac_pct_steam
Total_HVAC_cost = Elec + Gas + Steam + Fuel_Oil

Step 2: Apply ODCV savings percentage

Annual_savings = Total_HVAC_cost × ODCV%

Example: Building with $500K total energy, 45% to HVAC ($225K), 30% ODCV savings = $67,500/year.

ENERGY STAR Impact

Lower energy consumption improves ENERGY STAR score. We estimate post-ODCV scores using EPA methodology.

Technical Detail: ENERGY STAR Score Improvement

ENERGY STAR scores[10] compare a building's energy use to similar buildings nationwide. A score of 50 means median performance; 75+ qualifies for certification.

How scores are calculated:
EPA uses a gamma distribution regression model that maps source EUI to percentile rank, normalized for weather, operating hours, and building characteristics. See EPA Technical Reference[11].

Estimating post-ODCV score:

New_EUI = Current_EUI × (1 - ODCV% × Weighted_HVAC%)

Lower EUI → higher percentile → higher score. A 10% EUI reduction typically improves score by 5-15 points depending on starting position on the distribution curve.
